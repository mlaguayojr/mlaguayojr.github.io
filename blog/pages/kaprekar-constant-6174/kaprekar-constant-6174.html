What Is Kaprekar's Constant?

6174 is a magic number. Take any 4-digit number with at least two different digits, arrange them in descending order, then subtract the ascending arrangement. Repeat this process. No matter where you start, you will always reach 6174 in at most 7 steps. Once you hit 6174, the process loops forever — it maps back to itself.

This curious property was discovered by the Indian mathematician D.R. Kaprekar in 1949. The constant has since become a staple of recreational mathematics, demonstrating how structure and inevitability hide inside seemingly random numbers.

The Algorithm

The Kaprekar routine for 4-digit numbers is surprisingly simple:

  1. Take a number. Any 4-digit number (e.g., 1234). Pad with leading zeros if needed (e.g., 0050).
  2. Arrange descending. Sort the digits from largest to smallest (e.g., 1234 → 4321).
  3. Arrange ascending. Sort the digits from smallest to largest (e.g., 1234 → 1234).
  4. Subtract. Take descending minus ascending (e.g., 4321 − 1234 = 3087).
  5. Repeat. Use the result as your new starting number. Go back to step 1.

    Why Does It Work?

    The reason 6174 is so special lies in the mechanics of the process. There are only 10,000 possible 4-digit numbers. When you apply the Kaprekar routine, you create a directed graph where each number points to exactly one successor. With a finite space and one output per input, the system must eventually cycle — and it turns out there's only one cycle in this particular graph: 6174 → 6174.

    All other starting numbers funnel into this single fixed point. It's a small miracle of number theory, hiding in plain sight.
